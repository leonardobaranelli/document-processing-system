Natural language processing, or NLP, is the branch of artificial intelligence concerned with making computers understand, generate, and interact with human language. It draws from linguistics, computer science, and statistics, and it has progressed dramatically in the past decade thanks to deep learning. The goal of an NLP system is rarely to replicate human understanding exactly; rather, it is to extract useful structure from text so that software can answer questions, translate documents, classify messages, or summarize long reports.

At the lowest level, NLP begins with tokenization, which splits a text stream into meaningful units called tokens. Tokens can be words, subwords, or even characters depending on the task. After tokenization, many pipelines apply normalization steps such as lower-casing, removal of punctuation, and stemming or lemmatization, which reduce words to a common root form. These seemingly small choices have a large effect on the accuracy of downstream models.

Bag-of-words and TF-IDF were dominant text representations for many years. In a bag-of-words model, a document is represented as a vector where each dimension corresponds to a vocabulary term and each value is the count of that term. TF-IDF weights those counts by how informative a word is across the corpus. While simple, these representations are surprisingly effective for tasks like spam detection and topic classification.

Word embeddings revolutionized the field by turning individual words into dense real-valued vectors where semantic similarity is reflected by geometric proximity. Models like Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText learn embeddings from co-occurrence statistics. Encoders built on top of embeddings can then produce sentence- or document-level representations. Still, these static embeddings struggle with polysemy and context, which is why contextual models became popular.

Transformer architectures introduced in the late 2010s changed NLP once again. Self-attention lets a model weigh the importance of every other token in the sequence when producing a representation for a given token. This mechanism is highly parallelizable and captures long-range dependencies better than recurrent networks. Pretrained transformer models such as BERT, RoBERTa, T5, and GPT provide generic language understanding that can be fine-tuned for specific tasks with comparatively little labeled data.

Summarization is a particularly interesting NLP task for document processing systems. Extractive summarization selects the most informative sentences from the original text, while abstractive summarization generates new sentences that paraphrase the source. Extractive methods like TextRank treat sentences as nodes in a graph and rank them by a PageRank-inspired algorithm. More sophisticated models use sequence-to-sequence transformers trained on pairs of documents and human-written summaries. Both approaches have their place depending on the accuracy and resource constraints of the application.

Beyond summarization, NLP powers many features that modern software takes for granted. Sentiment analysis helps companies understand customer feedback. Named entity recognition identifies people, organizations, and locations in unstructured text. Question answering systems retrieve relevant passages and synthesize an answer. As these tools become more accessible, developers can integrate them into their products using only a handful of lightweight libraries or even models that run entirely on-device without sending data to external services.

Evaluating the quality of an NLP system remains an open research problem. Classical metrics like BLEU and ROUGE compare machine output against reference texts using n-gram overlap, which is fast but often correlates poorly with human judgment. Embedding-based metrics such as BERTScore and learned evaluators such as COMET attempt to capture semantic similarity, yet they can still be gamed or biased toward specific training distributions. For mission-critical applications, teams increasingly combine automated metrics with carefully designed human evaluation protocols, complemented by red-teaming exercises that probe the system for harmful, biased, or factually incorrect behavior before any release.

Multilingual and low-resource language support is another frontier where progress has been uneven. Most state-of-the-art models are trained predominantly on English, so their quality drops noticeably when applied to languages with smaller digital footprints. Techniques like cross-lingual transfer learning, multilingual pretraining, and the creation of open community datasets are helping to narrow this gap. Beyond the technical work, inclusive NLP requires collaboration with native speakers, linguists, and local institutions to ensure that the resulting tools respect cultural context rather than simply transplanting assumptions from dominant languages onto everyone else.
